The problem with deterministic tournament scoring
In the original Swiss Knife proposal, the alignment head (the "blade") produces a
deterministic scalar reward for each candidate token — call it
. The
tournament was designed so that every candidate plays every round and the
winner emerges through pairwise comparisons rather than a single argmax.
The problem: if every match uses the same fixed scores, the pairwise
comparisons add no information. A candidate that has the highest score beats
every opponent it faces, in every round, regardless of the bracket structure. The
tournament collapses to argmax selection. The elaborate Swiss pairing logic
becomes irrelevant.
Original architecture diagram:

For the tournament to do something the argmax cannot, the scoring function
must behave differently across matches. Two properties are needed:
●​ Stochasticity — the score of a candidate is a random variable, not a fixed
scalar. A lower-quality candidate can occasionally win a match.
●​ Intransitivity — because each match draws an independent random
functional, the win relation is not transitive. Candidate A can beat B, B can

beat C, and C can beat A. This is only meaningful in a pairwise tournament
where the bracket structure matters.
Both properties vanish the moment the same score vector is used for every
match.

The fix:
Instead of scoring candidates with a single fixed forward pass of the blade, we
define a family of stochastic scalar functionals of the blade's internal state. Before
each individual match in the tournament, a fresh member of this family is
sampled and a new random perturbation is applied to the blade's computation.
After the match, the perturbation is removed.

1. MC Dropout
Before the final linear projection, a fresh binary dropout mask is applied to the
hidden state vector :
Neurons zeroed by the mask contribute nothing to the logit distribution for this
match. The specific neurons zeroed are resampled independently for every
match.
What this tests: whether a candidate's reward advantage is robust to noise in the
final feature readout, or whether it is carried by a small set of co-firing hidden
neurons that may not consistently dominate in every match.

2. Random Projection
A random matrix (normalized) is drawn per match and used to perturb the
hidden state before projection:

This rotates and scales the representation in a fresh random direction for every
match, keeping the magnitude controlled via .
What this tests: whether the reward signal is stable under local linear
perturbations of the representation geometry.

3. Attention Head Subsampling
In each match, a random subset of attention heads in the final transformer
layers are silenced (their outputs are zeroed). The specific subset is redrawn per
match.
Different attention heads specialise in different kinds of context (e.g., syntactic
coherence, coreference, safety-relevant patterns).
By randomly varying which heads contribute to the reward signal, each match is
effectively judged by a different committee of "attentional criteria." A candidate
that is preferred regardless of which subset of heads is active has a more broadly
grounded alignment signal.
