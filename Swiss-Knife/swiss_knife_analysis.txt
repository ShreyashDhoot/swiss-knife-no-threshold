Executive Summary

This document re-examines the Swiss Knife proposal against the current decode-time alignment
literature (as of early 2026) and identies three issues with the draft framing, along with the
corrective path.
1.

The multi-head framing is not novel in itself. MOD (Shi et al., NeurIPS 2024), GenARM (Xu et al., ICLR 2025), PARM (2025), Collab (2025), and several others already operate in the frozen generator + swappable objective modules regime. The claim decode-time
alignment with modular heads that can hot-swap is taken.

2.

The genuine novelty is the tournament selection rule. Existing methods combine
objective scores either by linear mixture (MOD) or by argmax (ARGS, DeAL). Swiss Knife
proposes pairwise tournament aggregation, which is mechanically dierent and provides a specic defense against reward-maximisation degeneracies (refuse-always, boilerplate collapse).

3.

The speculative decoding framing in the abstract is currently aspirational. The
algorithm described in the plan document is Best-of-N with a tournament selector; it is not
speculative decoding in the sense of Leviathan et al. (2023). Two honest paths exist: (A) drop
the speculative framing and position as decode-time tournament alignment, or (B) actually
build the speculative-decoding-integrated version where the auditor replaces the verier. This
document recommends path B, and details it.

Key insight. The paper that works is: Tournament-based decode-time alignment, implemented as a replacement verier step inside a speculative-decoding loop, with a pluggable
objective blade. Every word in that sentence is doing load-bearing work, and the experiments must defend each.

4.3.2

Pairing rule in each round

1. Group candidates by current cumulative score.
2. Within each score group, pair adjacent candidates (by some secondary order  e.g., initial
draft rank).
3. If a score group has odd size, one candidate oats to pair with the closest candidate in the
next score group. This rarely matters for small K .
4. Avoid repeat pairings when possible (standard Swiss constraint).

4.3.3

Complexity

For K candidates and R rounds: R · K/2 matches total. Typical choice R = ⌈log2 K⌉:

 K = 4, R = 2: 4 matches (vs. knockout: 3).
 K = 8, R = 3: 12 matches (vs. knockout: 7).
 K = 16, R = 4: 32 matches (vs. knockout: 15).
Roughly 2× the matches of knockout, but considerably more robust to auditor noise because
every candidate's nal score reects multiple matches.
